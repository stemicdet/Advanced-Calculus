    Subsection A Brief History of the Derivative

    In the 17th century, Galileo discovered that the distance traveled by a falling object is proportional to the square of the time it has been falling. More precisely, if the distance fallen after \(t\) seconds is denoted \(s(t)\) and measured in meters, then
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} s(t) = 4.9t^2 \end{equation*}
    From this we can find the average velocity of a falling object over any time interval using the formula
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \text{Average Velocity} = \dfrac{\text{Change in Position}}{\text{Change in Time}} \end{equation*}
    Suppose however, that we wanted to find the instantaneous velocity of an object 3 seconds after we drop it.
    ğŸ”—
    The difficulty here is that we have a single instance of time instead of a time interval.
    ğŸ”—
    Isaac Newtonâ€™s approach to this problem was to approximate the instantaneous velocity by calculating the average velocity over smaller and smaller intervals.
    ğŸ”—
    The following table shows the average velocity over successively smaller time intervals.
    ğŸ”—
    It appears that as the time intervals get shorter, the average velocities get closer and closer to 29.4. Newton gave the following argument for why 29.4 is in fact the exact instantaneous velocity.
    ğŸ”—
    Let \(h\) be a small positive number. Then the average velocity of the falling object during the time interval \([3,3+h]\) is
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{align*} \frac{s(3+h)-s(3)}{3+h-3} \amp = \frac{4.9(3+h)^2-4.9(3)^2}{h} \\ \amp = \frac{4.9[9+6h+h^2-9]}{h} \\ \amp = \frac{29.4h+4.9h^2}{h}\\ \amp = 29.4+4.9h \end{align*}
    Newton then argued that since \(h\) is an arbitrarily small positive number, terms ``multiplied by it will be nothing in respect to the restâ€™â€™ and thus the exact instantaneous velocity is \(29.4\) \(m/s\text{.}\)

    ğŸ”—

    Subsection The Derivative

    The modern definition of the derivative is defined as follows.
    ğŸ”—

    Definition 2.

    Let \(g:A \to \mathbf{R}\) be a function defined on an interval \(A\text{.}\) Given \(c\in A\) the derivative of \(g\) at \(c\) is given by
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} g'(c) = \lim_{x\to c} \frac{g(x)-g(c)}{x-c} \end{equation*}
    provided this limit exists. In this case we say \(g\) is differentiable at \(c\text{.}\) If \(g'\) exists for all points \(c \in A\) we say that \(g\) is differentiable on \(A\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Hidden in this definition is the \(\epsilon\)-\(\delta\) relationship that comes from the way that the limit is defined. The \(\epsilon\)-\(\delta\) definition of the derivative looks like this.
    ğŸ”—

    Definition 3.

    Let \(g:A \to \mathbf{R}\) be a function defined on an interval \(A\text{.}\) Then the derivative of \(g\) at \(c \in A\) is \(L\) if for every \(\epsilon>0\text{,}\) there exists \(\delta>0\) such that
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \left| \frac{g(x)-g(c)}{x-c}-L \right| < \epsilon \end{equation*}
    whenever \(|x-c| < \delta\)
    ğŸ”—
    Note that by letting \(x = c+h\) the derivative can be rewritten as
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{g(c+h)-g(c)}{h} \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    In geometry a tangent line to a curve is a line that touches the curve and follows the same direction as the curve at the point of contact.
    ğŸ”—
    A secant line to a curve is a line that passes through 2 points on a curve.
    ğŸ”—
    The slope of the secant line on the curve \(f(x)\) that passes through points \((a,f(a))\) and \((b,f(b))\) is given by
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(a)-f(b)}{a-b} \end{equation*}
    As we take the limit as \(a\) approaches \(b\text{,}\) the secant line comes closer and closer to the tangent line at \(b\text{.}\) Thus the slope of the tangent line at \((b,f(b))\) is
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{a\to b} \frac{f(a)-f(b)}{a-b} = f'(b) \end{equation*}
    The example with the absolute value function shows that a continuous function may fail to be differentiable; however, every differentiable function must be continuous.
    ğŸ”—

    Theorem 5.

    If \(g: A \to \mathbf{R}\) is differentiable at a point \(c \in A\text{,}\) then \(g\) is continuous at \(c\) as well.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Proof.

    Since \(g\) is differentiable at \(c\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} g'(c) = \lim_{x\to c} \frac{g(x)-g(c)}{x-c} \end{equation*}
    exists.
    ğŸ”—
    We want to show \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c} g(x) = g(c)\text{.}\) By the Algebraic Limit Theorem for functional limits
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to c} (g(x)-g(c)) = \lim_{x\to c} \left( \frac{g(x)-g(c)}{x-c}\right)(x-c) = g'(c) \cdot 0 = 0. \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    It follows that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to c} g(x) = g(c). \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    A differentiable function must be continuous, but does the derivative, when viewed as a function of \(x\) have to be continuous?
    ğŸ”—

    Subsection Combinations of differentiable functions

    Theorem 8.

    Let \(f: A \to \mathbf{R}\) and \(g: B \to \mathbf{R}\) satisfy \(f(A) \subseteq B\) so that the composition \(g \circ f\) is defined. If \(f\) is differentiable at \(c \in A\) and if \(g\) is differentiable at \(f(c) \in B\text{,}\) then \(g \circ f\) is differentiable at \(c\) with \((g \circ f)'(c) = g'(f(c)) \cdot f'(c)\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Almost Proof:
    \begin{align*} (g\circ f)'(c) \amp = \lim_{x\to c} \frac{g(f(x))-g(f(c))}{x-c} \\ \amp =\lim_{x\to c} \frac{g(f(x))-g(f(c))}{f(x)-f(c)} \cdot \frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} \\ \amp =g'(f(c))\cdot f'(c) \end{align*}
    ğŸ”—
    The reason that the â€œalmost proofâ€ doesnâ€™t work as a rigorous proof is that it may be the case that in every \(\delta\) neighborhood of \(c\) there is an \(x\) such that \(f(x) = f(c)\) which causes division by zero. The proof will need to remedy this.
    ğŸ”—
    We will need the following result as a preliminary lemma for proving the Chain rule.

    Theorem 9. Caratheodoryâ€™s Theorem.

    Let \(f\) be defined on an interval \(I\) containing the point \(c\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is differentiable at \(c\) if and only if there exists a function \(\phi\) on \(I\) that is continuous at \(c\) and satisfies
    \begin{equation*} f(x) - f(c) = \phi(x) (x-c) \end{equation*}
    for \(x \in I\text{.}\) In this case we have \(\phi(c) = f'(c)\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Proof.

    For the forward direction, if \(f'(c)\) exists define \(\phi(x)\) by
    \begin{equation*} \phi(x) = \begin{cases} \hfill \frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} /amp \text{ if $x \neq c$} \\ \hfill f'(c) \hfill /amp \text{ if $x =c $} \\ \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    \(\phi(x)\) is continuous because
    \begin{equation*} f'(c) = \lim_{x\to c} \frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    Also \(f(x) - f(c) = \phi(x) (x-c)\) for all \(x \in I\text{.}\) Thus the forward direction is proved.
    ğŸ”—
    For the reverse direction, suppose that a function \(\phi\) exists that is continuous and \(f(x) - f(c) = \phi(x) (x-c)\) for all \(x \in I\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Then since \(\phi\) is continuous
    \begin{equation*} \phi(c) = \lim_{x\to c} \phi(x) = \lim_{x\to c} \frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} = f'(c) \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    Thus \(f'(c)\) exists and \(f'(c) = \phi(c)\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    We are now ready to present the proof of the chain rule.
    ğŸ”—

    Proof.

    Since \(f'(c)\) exists Caratheodoryâ€™s Theorem gives that there exists \(\phi(x)\) such that \(\phi\) is continuous at \(c\) and \(f(x)-f(c) = \phi(x)(x-c)\text{,}\) where \(\phi(c) = f'(c)\)
    ğŸ”—
    Since \(g'(f(c))\) exists Caratheodoryâ€™s Theorem gives that there exists \(\psi(x)\) such that \(\psi\) is continuous at \(d = f(c)\) and \(g(y)-g(d) = \psi(y)(y-d)\) for \(y \in I\) where \(\psi(d) = g'(d)\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Now substitute \(y = f(x)\) and \(d=f(c)\) to obtain
    \begin{equation*} g(f(x))-g(f(c)) = \psi(f(x)) (f(x)-f(c)) = \psi(f(x)) \phi(x) (x-c) \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    The function \(\psi(f(x)) \phi(x)\) is continuous at \(c\text{.}\) Hence by Caratheodoryâ€™s Theorem \(g(f(x))\) is differentiable at \(c\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Moreover, \(\psi(f(c)) \phi(c) = g'(f(c) f'(c)\) and hence \((g \circ f)'(x) = g'(f(c)) f'(c)\text{.}\)

    Subsection The Intermediate Value Property

    Recall the Intermediate Value Theorem for continuous functions.
    ğŸ”—
    Let \(f: [a,b] \to \mathbf{R}\) be continuous. If \(L\) is a real number satisfying \(f(a)< L < f(b)\) or \(f(a)\ge L \ge f(b)\text{,}\) then there exists a point \(c \in (a,b)\) such that \(f(c) = L\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    We say that a function \(g(x)\) (not necessarily continuous) satisfies the Intermediate Value Property if whenever \(L\) is a real number satisfying \(g(a)< L < g(b)\) or \(g(a)\ge L \ge g(b)\text{,}\) then there exists a point \(c \in (a,b)\) such that \(g(c) = L\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Example: Darbouxâ€™s function
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \begin{cases} \hfill x^2 \sin(1/x) \amp \text{ if $x \neq 0$} \\ \hfill 0 \hfill \amp \text{ if $x =0 $} \\ \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    is continuous and hence satisfies the Intermediate Value Property.
    ğŸ”—
    The derivative of Darbouxâ€™s function is not continuous at \(0\text{;}\) however, it still satisfies the Intermediate Value Property.
    ğŸ”—
    In fact, Darboux proved that every derivative satisfies the Intermediate Value Property.
    ğŸ”—
    The proof that every derivative satisfies the Intermediate Value Property follows from one of the most important uses of the derivative, determining the maximum or minimum values of a function.
    ğŸ”—

    Theorem 11. Interior Extremum Theorem.

    Let \(f\) be differentiable on an open interval \((a,b)\text{.}\) If \(f\) attains a maximum value at some point \(c \in (a,b)\) (i.e., \(f(c)\geq f(x)\) for all \(x \in (a,b)\)) then \(f'(c) = 0\text{.}\) The same is true if \(f(c)\) is a minimum value.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Proof.

    Since \(c\) is a maximum, \(f(x)-f(c) <q 0\) and hence
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to c^+} \frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} <q 0 \end{equation*}
    while
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to c^-} \frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} \geq 0. \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    Since \(f'(c)\) exists, both one sided limits exist and equal \(f'(c)\text{.}\) Hence \(f'(c) = 0\text{.}\) The proof when \(c\) is a minimum is similar.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Theorem 13. Darbouxâ€™s Theorem (AKA IVT for derivatives).

    If \(f\) is differentiable on an interval \([a,b]\) and if \(\alpha\) satisfies \(f'(a) < \alpha < f'(b)\) (or \(f'(b) < \alpha < f'(a)\)), then there exists a point \(c \in (a,b)\) where \(f'(c) = \alpha\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Proof.

    Let \(g(x) =f(x)- \alpha x\text{.}\) Then \(g\) is differentiable on \([a,b]\) and \(g'(x) = f'(x)-\alpha\text{.}\) Also \(g'(a) < 0 < g'(b)\) and we want to show that there exists \(c \in (a,b)\) where \(g'(c) = 0\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Since \(g'(a) < 0\text{,}\) it follows from the lemma that the minimum of \(g\) does not occur at \(a\text{.}\) Likewise since \(g'(b) > 0\) it also follows that the minimum of \(g\) does not occur at \(b\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Thus it must be that the minimum of \(g\) occurs at \(c\in (a,b)\text{.}\) Hence by the interior extremum theorem \(f'(c) = 0\) and this completes the proof.
