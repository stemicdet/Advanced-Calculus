    Section Week 6: Power Series (6.5)

    Subsection The Binomial Series

    The Binomial Theorem demonstrates the correct way to expand the powers of a binomial.
    \begin{equation*} (y+x)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} x^{k} y^{n-k} \end{equation*}
    where \(\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!k!}\)
    ðŸ”—
    It is sometimes written as the special case
    \begin{equation*} (1+x)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} x^{k} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Around 1665 Newton generalized the Binomial Theorem for the case when \(n\) is not an integer. When this happens the sum becomes an infinite series.
    \begin{equation*} (1+x)^{\alpha} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{\alpha}{k} x^k \end{equation*} \end{equation*}
    where \(\binom{\alpha}{k} = \frac{\alpha(\alpha-1)(\alpha-2) \ldots (\alpha-k+1)}{k!}\)
    ðŸ”—
    However, Newton never provided a proof of this result!
    ðŸ”—
    In his 1821 book Cours dâ€™analyse Cauchy gave a proof that the binomial series converges to \((1+x)^{\alpha}\text{.}\) Abel was interested in further generalizing the Binomial Theorem to complex exponents and carefully studied Cauchyâ€™s proof, which led him to discover a flaw in the proof.
    ðŸ”—
    Cauchyâ€™s proof relies on the fact that \(\phi(x) = \displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{\alpha}{k} x^k\) is a continuous function on \((-1,1)\text{,}\) which he claimed followed because the limit of a sum of continuous functions is continuous.
    ðŸ”—
    In an 1826 paper Abel presented an example that the showed that the limit of a sum of continuous functions is not always continuous and thus Cauchyâ€™s proof is incomplete unless the sum converges uniformly. Abel also showed a way to fix Cauchyâ€™s proof if this isnâ€™t the case.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Power Series

    To determine whether the Binomials series is uniformly convergent or not we need to build up some theory involving power series. A power series is a function of the form
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n = a_0 + a_1x + a_2x^2 + a_3x^3 + \ldots \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The binomial series is an example of a power series.
    ðŸ”—
    Another example is the series
    \begin{equation*} g(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n} = x+\frac{x^2}{2}+\frac{x^3}{3}+ \ldots \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Show that \(\displaystyle g(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n} \) converges when \(|x| < 1\text{.}\) What happens if \(x =1\text{?}\) What if \(x = -1\text{?}\)
    ðŸ”—
    In the previous example, the set of points where the series converges was the interval \([-1,1)\text{.}\) As it turns out the set where a power series converges is always an interval centered at \(0\) or the single point \(\{0\}\text{.}\)In the previous example, the set of points where the series converges was the interval \([-1,1)\text{.}\) As it turns out the set where a power series converges is always an interval centered at \(0\) or the single point \(\{0\}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 43.

    If a power series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n\) converges at some point \(x_0 \in \mathbf{R}\text{,}\) then it converges absolutely for any \(x\) satisfying \(|x| < |x_0|\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x_0^n\) converges. Then the sequence of terms \((a_n x_0^n)\) converges to \(0\) and thus is bounded.
    ðŸ”—
    Hence there exists \(M>0\) such that \(|a_n x_0^n| < M\) for all \(n \in \mathbf{N}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose \(|x|< |x_0|\text{.}\) Then,
    \begin{equation*} |a_n x^n| = |a_n x_0^n| \left| \frac{x}{x_0} \right|^n \leq M \left| \frac{x}{x_0} \right|^n. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Hence it follows from the comparison test that
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} |a_n x^n| \leq \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} M \left| \frac{x}{x_0} \right|^n. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Since \(|x| < |x_0|\) this is a geometric series with \(r = \left|\frac{x}{x_0} \right| < 1\) and thus converges.
    ðŸ”—
    Hence \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} |a_n x^n|\) converges absolutely.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    As previously mentioned a consequence of this theorem is that the set of points where a power series converges is either an interval of the form \((-R,R)\text{,}\) \([-R, R]\text{,}\) \((-R,R]\) or \([-R,R)\) or all \(\mathbf{R}\) or \(\{0\}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    This set is referred to as the interval of convergence of the power series and the number \(R\) is called the radius of convergence.
    ðŸ”—
    When the interval of convergence is \(\mathbf{R}\) the radius of convergence is said to be \(\infty\) and when the interval of convergence is \(\{0\}\text{,}\) the radius of convergence is said to be \(0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection The Ratio Test

    The Ratio Test is a test for series convergence which is useful for determining the radius of convergence of power series.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 44. The Ratio Test.

    Let \((a_n)\) be a sequence of nonzero real numbers.
    ðŸ”—
    1. If there exists \(r \in \mathbf{R}\) with \(0 < r < 1\) and \(k \in \mathbf{N}\) such that \(\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right| \leq r\) for \(n \geq k\text{,}\) then the series \(\sum a_n\) is absolutely convergent.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. If there exists \(k \in \mathbf{N}\) such that \(\left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| \geq 1\) for \(n \geq k\text{,}\) then the series \(\sum a_n\) is divergent.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    We first prove part 1. Suppose \(\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right| \leq r\) for \(n \geq k\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{align*} \amp |a_{k+1}| \leq r |a_k|\\ \amp |a_{k+2}| \leq r|a_{k+1}| \leq r^2 |a_k|\\ \amp \leq r|a_{k+m-1}| \leq r^2 |a_{k+m-2}| \ldots \leq r^m |a_k| \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    Thus
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} |a_n| \leq \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} r^n |a_k| \end{equation*}
    and since this is a geometric series with \(r < 1\text{,}\) \(\sum a_n\) converges absolutely by the comparison test.
    ðŸ”—
    Now we prove part 2. Suppose \(\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right| \geq 1\) for \(n \geq k\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{align*} \amp |a_{k+1}| \geq |a_k| \\ \amp |a_{k+2}| \geq |a_{k+1}| \geq |a_k| \\ \amp |a_{k+m}| \geq |a_{k+m-1}| \geq |a_{k+m-2}| \ldots \geq |a_k| \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    Thus \(\displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} |a_n| \geq |a_k| > 0\) and thus the series \(\sum a_n\) diverges.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Corollary 45.

    Let \((a_n)\) be a nonzero sequence in \(\mathbf{R}\) and suppose that the limit \(r = \displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{a{n+1}}{a_n}\right|\) exists. Then \(\sum a_n\) is absolutely convergent when \(r < 1\) and divergent when \(r > 1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    If \(r=1\) then the ratio test is inconclusive. The series may converge absolutely, converge conditionally, or diverge.
    ðŸ”—
    Example: Find the interval of convergence and radius of convergence of the following power series.
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{3^n x^n}{n^2} \end{equation*}

    Example: Find the interval of convergence and radius of convergence of the following power series.
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!} \end{equation*}

    Subsection Uniform Convergence

    Letâ€™s consider again the problem of proving that the Binomial series converges. Cauchy showed that if the series
    \begin{equation*} \phi(x) = \displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{\alpha}{k} x^k \end{equation*}
    is a continuous function then it must converge to \((1+x)^{\alpha}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We can show that \(\phi(x)\) is continuous by showing that the convergence is uniform.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 46.

    If a power series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n\) converges absolutely at a point \(x_0\text{,}\) then it converges uniformly on the closed interval \([-c,c]\) where \(c = |x_0|\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Recall the Weierstrass M-test states that if \(|f_n| < M_n\) and \(\sum M_n\) converges then \(\sum f_n\) converges uniformly.
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose \(x \in [-c,c]\text{.}\) Then \(|a_n x^n| \leq |a_n| |x_0|^n\) and since \(\sum a_n x_0^n\) converges absolutely, it follows from the M-test that \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n\) converges uniformly.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Show that the Binomial Series \(\phi(x) = \displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{\alpha}{k} x^k\) converges absolutely (and hence uniformly) when \(|x| < 1\) and diverges when \(|x| < 1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    It thus follows that \(\phi(x) = \displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{\alpha}{k} x^k\) is continuous on \((-1,1)\) and thus by Cauchyâ€™s argument it converges to \((1+x)^{\alpha}\) there.
    ðŸ”—
    We also know that \(\phi(x)\) is undefined when \(|x|>1\) because the series diverges. This leaves open the question of what happens at the endpoints \(1\) and \(-1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    In particular we want to know if \(\phi(x)\) is continuous at these endpoints and thus converges to \((1+x)^{\alpha}\) there as well.
    ðŸ”—
    Whether the Binomial series converges at the endpoints depends on the value of \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    If \(\alpha \geq 0\) then the series converges absolutely at both endpoints. Hence the converges is uniform and \(\phi(x)\) is continuous at both endpoints.
    ðŸ”—
    If \(-1 < \alpha < 0 \) then the series converges conditionally at \(1\) and diverges at \(-1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    If \(\alpha \leq -1\) the series diverges. Hence \(\phi(x)\) is undefined at the endpoints.
    ðŸ”—
    If the Binomial series converges conditionally then we canâ€™t use the Absolute Convergence Implies Uniform Convergence Theorem to show that the Binomial series converges uniformly at \(1\text{.}\) Instead we will need to use a theorem due to Abel to show that convergence at a point implies uniform convergence there as well.
    ðŸ”—
    Abel developed this theorem specifically to fix the flaw in Cauchyâ€™s proof that the Binomial series converges to \((1+x)^{\alpha}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Lemma 47. Abelâ€™s Lemma.

    Let \(B_n\) satisfy \(B_1 \geq B_2 \geq B_3 \geq \ldots \geq 0\text{,}\) and let \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\) be a series for which the partial sums are bounded. In other words, suppose there exists \(A > 0\) such that
    \begin{equation*} |A_1+A_2+ \ldots + A_n| \leq A \end{equation*}
    for all \(n \in \mathbf{N}\text{.}\) Then for all \(n \in \mathbf{N}\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} |A_1 B_1 + A_2 B_2 + A_3 B_3 + \ldots + A_n B_n| \leq A B_1. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 48. Abelâ€™s Theorem.

    Let \(g(x) = \displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n\) be a power series that converges at the point \(x = R > 0\text{.}\) Then the series converges uniformly on the interval \([0,R]\text{.}\) A similar result holds if the series converges at \(x=-R\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Because we will want to make use of Abelâ€™s Lemma, we first write
    \begin{equation*} g(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (a_n R_n) \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^n \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Let \(\epsilon > 0\text{.}\) It follows from the Cauchy criterion that the series converges uniformly if it can be shown that there exists \(N \in \mathbf{N}\) such that whenever \(n > m \geq N\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \left|(a_{m+1} R_{m+1}) \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^{m+1} + (a_{m+2} R_{m+2}) \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^{m+2} + \ldots + (a_n R_n) \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^n \right| < \epsilon. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The hypothesis of the theorem is that \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n R^n\) converges. Hence by the Cauchy criterion there exists \(N \in \mathbf{N}\) such that for \(n > m \geq N\)
    \begin{equation*} |a_{m+1}R^{m+1} + a_{m+2}R^{m+2} + \ldots + a_n R^n| < \epsilon. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    We can thus apply Abelâ€™s Lemma to
    \begin{equation*} \left|(a_{m+1} R_{m+1}) \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^{m+1} + (a_{m+2} R_{m+2}) \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^{m+2} + \ldots + (a_n R_n) \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^n \right| \end{equation*}
    with \(A_n = a_n R^n\) and \(B_n = \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^n\)
    ðŸ”—
    This gives
    \begin{align*} \left|(a_{m+1} R_{m+1}) \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^{m+1} + (a_{m+2} R_{m+2}) \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^{m+2} + \ldots + (a_n R_n) \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^n \right| \amp \leq \epsilon \left( \frac{x}{R} \right)^{m+1} \\ \amp \leq \epsilon \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    If \(-1< \alpha < 0\) the Binomial series converges conditionally at \(x=1\text{.}\) Abelâ€™s Theorem tells us that it converges uniformly on \((-1,1]\) and thus \(\phi(x)= \displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{\alpha}{k} x^k\) is continuous at \(1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Thus \(\phi(x)\) converges to \((1+x)^{\alpha}\) on \((-1,1]\) and in particular at \(x=1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The following Theorem summarizes our conclusions about when a power series is a continuous function.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 49.

    Suppose
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n \end{equation*}
    converges (pointwise) on an interval \(A \subseteq \mathbf{R}\text{.}\) Then the function \(f\) is continuous on \(A\text{.}\)

    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Derivatives of Power Series

    Suppose that \(f(x) = \displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n\) defines a continuous function on a set \(A \subseteq \mathbf{R}\text{.}\) Is \(f\) differentiable on \(A\) and if so what is \(f'(x)\text{?}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We first observe that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx} f(x) = \frac{d}{dx} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{d}{dx} a_n x^n = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n a_n x^{n-1} \end{equation*}
    provided that the interchange between the differential operator \(\frac{d}{dx}\) and the sum is permitted.
    ðŸ”—
    We will justify the prior calculations by appealing to the Term-by-term differentiability Theorem. Recall that this required the original series to converge and the series of derivatives to converge uniformly.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 50.

    If \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n\) converges for all \(x \in (-R,R)\) then the differentiated series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n a_n x^{n-1}\) converges at each \(x \in (-R,R)\) as well. Consequently the convergence is uniform on compact sets contained in \((-R,R)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Choose \(x \in (-R,R)\text{.}\) Then there exists \(t\) such that \(|x|< t < R\text{.}\) Since \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n t^n\) converges, it follows from the ratio test that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}t^{n+1}}{a_n t^n}\right| \leq 1 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Hence it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| |t| \leq 1 \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| \leq \frac{1}{|t|} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Now we apply the ratio test to the series \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}n a_n x^{n-1}\)
    \begin{align*} \lim_{n\to \infty} \left| \frac{(n+1)a_{n+1}x^{n+1}}{n a_n x^n}\right| \leq \amp \lim_{n\to \infty} \left| \frac{n+1}{n} \right| \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{ a_n}\right| |x| \\ \amp \leq \left| \frac{1}{ t}\right| |x| \\ \amp < 1 \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    Hence it follows from the ratio test that \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}n a_n x^{n-1}\) converges absolutely at \(x\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Combining the previous result with the Term-by-term differentiability Theorem gives us the following conditions for a power series to be differentiable.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 51.

    Suppose \(f(x) = \displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n\) converges on an interval \(A \subseteq \mathbf{R}\text{.}\) The function \(f\) is continuous on \(A\) and differentiable on any open interval \((-R,R) \subseteq A\text{.}\) The derivative is given by
    \begin{equation*} f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n a_n x^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Moreover, \(f\) is infinitely differentiable on \((-R,R)\) and the successive derivatives can be obtained via term-by-term differentiation of the appropriate series.
